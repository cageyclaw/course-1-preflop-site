# RFI (Raise First In)

## Intro
**RFI (Raise First In)** means you are the **first player to enter the pot with a raise** after everyone folds to you.

If you want a boring-but-profitable preflop foundation, this is it.

RFI is the baseline that quietly controls:
- how often you win the blinds uncontested
- how often you face 3-bets
- what kinds of postflop situations you create (in position vs out of position, shallow vs deep, etc.)

This course uses charts as **structure, not a prison**. We’re building an automatic baseline you can execute fast, then adjust intelligently.

---

## Why RFI matters (more than you think)
RFI isn’t just “what hands do I open?” It determines your whole decision tree.

When you RFI well:
- Your opponents can’t easily exploit your position leaks (“too loose early” / “too tight late”)
- Your ranges are coherent (value + playable bluffs)
- You generate “free” EV from folds (especially late position)
- You avoid the common trap: playing too many weak hands out of position

When you RFI poorly:
- You donate chips with hands that realize equity badly (especially early position)
- You get 3-bet into miserable folds too often
- You end up defending too many marginal postflop spots because you started with junk

## Defaults (use these until you have a reason to change them)

### Stack depth default (for this course)
Unless a lesson says otherwise, assume **100bb** effective stacks as the default learning environment.

If your most common game is different (e.g., tournaments at 30–50bb), still learn the 100bb baseline first—then we’ll show how to compress/expand later.

### Open size default
Use a consistent open size to avoid accidental “tells”:
- **Default RFI size: 2.5x** (e.g., blinds 100/200 → open to 500)

**Adjustments (simple, optional):**
- If stacks are shallow and play is aggressive: you can trend smaller (e.g., 2.0–2.2x) to reduce risk
- If players call too wide and don’t 3-bet enough: you can trend larger (e.g., 2.7–3.0x) to tax calls  
But don’t yo-yo your sizing based on hand strength. Same size for the whole range.

---

## Key rule #1: Position is the main dial
Your position decides how wide you can open—because it decides how often you’ll be **in position postflop** and how often you’ll face pressure from players behind.

![Position widening ladder](/course-md/position-ladder.svg)

**Guardrails (non-negotiable basics):**
- **UTG / EP:** tight and disciplined (you’ll be out of position a lot and face stronger ranges behind)
- **HJ / MP:** widen slightly (still needs solid playability)
- **CO:** widen again (more steals, more position)
- **BTN:** widest opens (you win more pots from folds and position)
- **SB:** tricky (you’re out of position the whole hand); you need a clear plan and tighter discipline than BTN

**Simple mental model:**  
Early position is a job interview. Button is a party. Small blind is a long commute to work.

---

## Key rule #2: Learn the “bottom of range” (not every combo)
Trying to memorize every hand is slow and fragile.

Instead, learn the **weakest opens** (“bottom-of-range” hands) for each position. If you know:
- what the borderline opens are, and
- what the borderline folds are,  
…you can reconstruct the rest naturally.

**How to use charts correctly:**
- Treat them like guardrails: “this is roughly where the fence is”
- Use them to build speed and consistency
- Don’t treat them like a magic spell that overrides obvious table dynamics

---

## Key rule #3: Your range must be defendable
A good RFI range is not “hands I like.” It’s hands that:
- can handle calls without bleeding EV
- can continue vs 3-bets in a realistic way (at least sometimes)
- aren’t dominated constantly (especially offsuit broadways and weak kings/queens in early seats)

**Red flag:** if you open a hand and your plan versus a 3-bet is “I guess I fold,” you might be opening too thin (especially early).

## Examples (what “position changes everything” looks like)

### Example 1 — Same hand, different seat
You’re dealt **K9 suited**.
- **UTG:** often a **fold** (domination + poor realization when called / 3-bet)
- **CO/BTN:** often an **open** (position lets you realize equity and win blinds)

What changed? Not the cards. **The seat.**

### Example 2 — Button steals (when blinds over-fold)
If the blinds fold too much, your BTN RFI doesn’t need to “hit” postflop to be profitable—you win preflop often.

**Practical adjustment:** widen your BTN opens a bit (but keep your sizing consistent).

### Example 3 — When you should not auto-steal
If the blinds:
- defend wide
- call and don’t fold postflop
- or 3-bet aggressively  
…then the weakest opens lose value fast.

**Practical adjustment:** tighten the bottom of your range, especially offsuit trash, and value-open a bit more.

## Common mistakes (and how to stop doing them)

### 1) Opening the same range from every position
That’s not “fearless poker.” That’s donating chips.

**Fix:** tighten early, widen late. Make it automatic.

### 2) Changing open size based on hand strength
If you go bigger with premiums and smaller with junk, you’re basically sending a newsletter.

**Fix:** pick a default size and use it for your entire RFI range.

### 3) Over-valuing “it’s suited”
Suited helps, but it doesn’t magically make a hand good from early position.

**Fix:** in EP, prioritize hands that make strong top pair / strong high-card value and can continue vs pressure.

### 4) Ignoring who’s behind you
Your RFI is not just about you—it’s about who can punish you.

**Fix:** if aggressive 3-bettors are behind, tighten your marginal opens (especially in CO/HJ). If passive nits are behind, you can open wider.

### 5) Playing SB like it’s the Button
The SB is not a discount BTN. You’re out of position forever.

**Fix:** use a clear SB strategy (we’ll define it in the SB module). Until then: be disciplined.

## Drills (build the habit fast)

### The 10-minute RFI drill (daily)
1. Pick **one position** (start with BTN or CO).
2. Lock one stack depth (default: **100bb**).
3. Run **50–100 “folded to you” reps** and record:
   - your open/fold decision
   - the 3–5 hands you were least confident about (your “bottom-of-range stress tests”)

**Goal:** speed + accuracy, not hero creativity.

### Bottom-of-range flash drill (3 minutes)
For one position, write:
- 5 hands that are **clear opens**
- 5 hands that are **clear folds**
- 5 hands that are **borderline** (the real learning zone)

Repeat daily until it feels boring. Boring is good.

## RFI decision checklist (copy/paste)
Before you click “Raise”:
- What position am I in?
- Who’s left to act (especially aggressive 3-bettors)?
- Who’s in the blinds (over-folders vs sticky defenders)?
- Am I using my **default open size**?
- Is this hand near my **bottom of range**? If yes, be extra honest.

## Quick takeaway
RFI is the simplest preflop skill with the biggest compounding payoff:
- **tight early**
- **wide late**
- **consistent sizing**
- **learn the bottom of range**
- **adjust to who’s behind and who’s in the blinds**

That’s the foundation. Everything else stacks on top of it.
